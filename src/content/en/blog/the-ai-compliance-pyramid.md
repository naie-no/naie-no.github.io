*June 8, 2026 – Wessel Braakman*

Over the past year, I've spent a lot of time following discussions around AI governance, regulation, certifications, and audits. Everyone seems to be talking about the same questions:

How do we regulate AI?
How do we ensure compliance?
How do we certify systems as trustworthy?

What I struggled to find was a clear explanation of what those activities are actually built upon. 

While thinking about where NAIE fits within the broader AI ecosystem, I developed a simple model that I call the AI Compliance Pyramid.

## Tier 1: The EU AI Act

![Tier 1](/blog/AI-Compliance-Pyramid-Tier-1.png)

At the very top sits the EU. The AI Act and other regulatory initiatives are driving much of the conversation around responsible AI.

## Tier 2: National & local authorities

![Tier 2](/blog/AI-Compliance-Pyramid-Tier-1&2.png)

Below that sits Regulation and Supervision. Laws are only effective when there are mechanisms to interpret, enforce, and oversee them.

## Tier 3: Third parties & consultants

![Tier 3](/blog/AI-Compliance-Pyramid-Tier-1&2&3.png)

Underneath that sits Audit and Guidance. Organizations need practical ways to assess whether systems actually comply with requirements. Auditors need methodologies. Companies need guidance. Standards need interpretation.

So far, this is where most conversations tend to stop.
But while looking at this structure, I kept asking myself a different question:

What are audits actually based on?

An auditor cannot simply declare a system fair.
A regulator cannot simply declare a system unbiased.
A certification body cannot simply declare a system trustworthy.
All of these activities require evidence.

And that realization led me to the bottom layer of the pyramid.

## Tier 4: Base data & benchmarks.

![Tier 4](/blog/AI-Compliance-Pyramid-Tier-1&2&3&4.png)

Without datasets, benchmarks, evaluation methods, and measurable criteria, the layers above have very little to stand on. Audits become opinions. Guidance becomes difficult to validate. Regulation becomes difficult to operationalize.

The more I thought about it, the more it felt like this bottom layer is a missing piece in many AI governance discussions.

And that is also where I believe NAIE currently fits.
We are not a regulator.
We are not a certification body.
We are not an auditing firm.

Instead, we focus on helping build the evidence layer that makes the rest possible: localized benchmarks, measurement frameworks, and datasets that allow AI systems to be evaluated in a meaningful way.

![Pyramid](/blog/AI-Compliance-Pyramid.png)

In other words, while much of the industry is focused on the upper layers of the pyramid, we are working on the foundation.

Over the coming articles, I want to explore each layer of this pyramid in more detail, starting at the top and working our way down to the place where trustworthy AI ultimately begins: the evidence itself.
